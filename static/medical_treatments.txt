1. Viral Fever (General / Common Viral Infection)
Cause: Viruses (e.g., influenza, rhinovirus, adenovirus).
Symptoms: Fever, body aches, fatigue, headache, sore throat, cough.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Primary: Paracetamol (acetaminophen) 500–650 mg every 6 hours (max 4g/day).
  - Supportive: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS), electrolyte fluids, multivitamins, Zinc 20 mg daily.
  - Avoid: Aspirin/NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) if dengue suspected.
Cure: Self-limiting (resolves in 3–7 days).
Precautions:
  - Stay hydrated with water, ORS, fruit juices.
  - Light diet (soups, rice, fruits).
  - Avoid contact with others to prevent spread.
When to see a doctor: Fever >3 days, rash, bleeding, confusion, severe dehydration.

2. Malaria
Cause: Plasmodium parasites (P. falciparum, P. vivax, etc.) via Anopheles mosquitoes.
Symptoms: High fever, chills, sweating, headache, muscle pain, nausea.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Uncomplicated: Artemether-Lumefantrine 80/480 mg twice daily for 3 days or Artesunate-Amodiaquine for 3 days.
  - P. vivax/P. ovale: Chloroquine 25 mg/kg over 3 days + Primaquine 15 mg daily for 14 days.
  - Severe: IV Artesunate 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24 hours, then daily, followed by ACT.
  - Supportive: Paracetamol for fever, hydration.
Cure: Curable with timely treatment; relapse possible in P. vivax/ovale.
Precautions:
  - Use insecticide-treated bed nets.
  - Apply DEET-based repellents.
  - Chemoprophylaxis (Mefloquine, Doxycycline) in endemic areas.
  - Eliminate stagnant water to reduce mosquito breeding.

3. Dengue Fever
Cause: Dengue virus (serotypes 1–4) via Aedes mosquitoes.
Symptoms: High fever, severe headache, joint/muscle pain, rash, mild bleeding (e.g., nosebleeds).
Treatment/Medication:
  - Symptomatic: Paracetamol 500 mg every 6 hours for fever/pain.
  - Avoid: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) due to bleeding risk.
  - Fluids: ORS, coconut water, soups for hydration.
  - Monitor: Platelet count, bleeding gums, abdominal pain.
Cure: Self-limiting; severe dengue requires hospitalization.
Precautions:
  - Use mosquito repellents, long-sleeved clothing.
  - Eliminate standing water to prevent mosquito breeding.
  - Seek care for warning signs (persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, bleeding).

4. Typhoid Fever
Cause: Salmonella typhi bacteria from contaminated food/water.
Symptoms: Prolonged fever, weakness, abdominal pain, constipation/diarrhea, loss of appetite.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antibiotics: Azithromycin 500 mg once daily for 7 days or Cefixime 200 mg twice daily for 10 days.
  - Severe cases: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily for 10–14 days.
  - Supportive: Hydration, light meals, paracetamol for fever.
Cure: Curable with antibiotics; relapse possible if untreated.
Precautions:
  - Typhoid vaccine (oral/injectable) before travel to endemic areas.
  - Drink boiled/bottled water.
  - Practice hand hygiene and food safety.

5. COVID-19 (Mild/Moderate)
Cause: SARS-CoV-2 virus.
Symptoms: Fever, cough, fatigue, loss of taste/smell, shortness of breath, sore throat.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Mild: Paracetamol 500 mg for fever, Cetirizine 10 mg daily for cold symptoms, Vitamin C 1000 mg, Zinc 20 mg daily.
  - Moderate/severe: Oxygen therapy, corticosteroids (Dexamethasone), antivirals (Paxlovid) under supervision.
Cure: Most recover in 1–2 weeks; severe cases may take longer.
Precautions:
  - Vaccination (primary + boosters).
  - Wear masks in crowded areas.
  - Practice hand hygiene, social distancing.

6. Common Cold / Flu
Cause: Rhinoviruses, coronaviruses, influenza viruses (A, B, C).
Symptoms: Runny nose, sneezing, sore throat, cough, mild fever.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Symptomatic: Paracetamol for fever/pain, Cetirizine or Loratadine for sneezing/runny nose, Ambroxol + Guaifenesin cough syrup twice daily.
  - Supportive: Steam inhalation, saline nasal drops, hydration.
  - Avoid: Antibiotics unless bacterial infection confirmed.
Cure: Self-limiting (7–10 days).
Precautions:
  - Wash hands frequently.
  - Avoid touching face or sharing utensils.
  - Boost immunity with vitamin C-rich foods.

7. Diarrhea / Gastroenteritis
Cause: Viruses (norovirus), bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella), or parasites.
Symptoms: Loose stools, abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, dehydration.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Rehydration: ORS (1 liter per 3–4 stools in adults).
  - Zinc: 10–20 mg/day for 10–14 days (children).
  - Antibiotics (bacterial): Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 3 days or Azithromycin (prescribed).
  - Avoid: Antidiarrheal drugs unless severe.
Cure: Resolves with rehydration; antibiotics for bacterial causes.
Precautions:
  - Drink safe water, eat hygienically prepared food.
  - Follow BRAT diet (bananas, rice, applesauce, toast).
  - Avoid antidiarrheal drugs unless prescribed.

8. Acidity / GERD
Cause: Stomach acid reflux into esophagus.
Symptoms: Heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain, difficulty swallowing.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antacids: Aluminum hydroxide, magnesium hydroxide (Digene syrup as needed).
  - PPIs: Pantoprazole 40 mg once daily before breakfast.
  - H2 blockers: Ranitidine, Famotidine.
Cure: Manageable; symptoms resolve with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Avoid spicy, fatty, acidic foods, late-night meals, smoking, alcohol.
  - Eat smaller meals, avoid lying down after eating.
  - Maintain healthy weight.

9. Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
Cause: Bacteria (E. coli) entering the urinary tract.
Symptoms: Burning urination, frequent urination, cloudy urine, pelvic pain.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antibiotics: Nitrofurantoin 100 mg twice daily for 5 days or Fosfomycin 3 g single dose.
  - Pain relief: Phenazopyridine 200 mg thrice daily for 2 days (short-term).
  - Supportive: Drink >2 liters water daily.
Cure: Curable with antibiotics; recurrent cases need evaluation.
Precautions:
  - Maintain hygiene (wipe front to back).
  - Urinate after sexual activity.
  - Avoid holding urine, tight clothing.

10. Diabetes Mellitus (Type 2)
Cause: Insulin resistance due to genetics, obesity, sedentary lifestyle.
Symptoms: Increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision.
Treatment/Medication:
  - First-line: Metformin 500–1000 mg twice daily.
  - Add-on: Glimepiride, Dapagliflozin, Sitagliptin.
  - Severe: Insulin therapy (e.g., 10 units basal + correction doses).
  - Lifestyle: Low-carb diet, exercise, glucose monitoring.
Cure: Manageable; remission possible with lifestyle changes.
Precautions:
  - Monitor blood glucose regularly.
  - Avoid sugary foods/drinks.
  - Regular check-ups for complications (retinopathy, neuropathy).

11. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)
Cause: Genetics, stress, poor diet, inactivity, underlying conditions.
Symptoms: Often asymptomatic; may include headaches, dizziness, nosebleeds.
Treatment/Medication:
  - First-line: Amlodipine 5 mg daily, Losartan 50 mg daily, Hydrochlorothiazide 12.5–25 mg daily.
  - Lifestyle: Low-salt diet, exercise, quit smoking.
  - Goal: BP <130/80 mmHg.
Cure: Manageable but not curable.
Precautions:
  - Monitor BP regularly.
  - Avoid smoking, excessive alcohol, processed foods.
  - Maintain healthy weight.

12. Asthma
Cause: Airway inflammation from allergens, exercise, infections.
Symptoms: Wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness, cough.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Reliever: Salbutamol inhaler 2 puffs as needed.
  - Controller: Budesonide inhaler twice daily.
  - Severe attack: Nebulization with salbutamol + ipratropium, oral steroids (prescribed).
Cure: Manageable but not curable.
Precautions:
  - Avoid triggers (dust, pollen, smoke, strong perfumes).
  - Use controller inhaler regularly.
  - Carry reliever inhaler.

13. Anemia
Cause: Iron, vitamin B12, folate deficiency; chronic diseases; blood loss.
Symptoms: Fatigue, pale skin, shortness of breath, dizziness.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Iron deficiency: Ferrous sulfate 325 mg 1–2 times daily, Folic acid 5 mg daily, Vitamin C to aid absorption.
  - B12 deficiency: Cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg IM weekly × 4, then monthly or 1000 mcg oral daily.
Cure: Curable if deficiency-related; chronic causes need management.
Precautions:
  - Eat iron-rich foods (spinach, red meat, lentils).
  - Avoid tea/coffee with iron supplements.
  - Regular blood tests.

14. Migraine
Cause: Neurological condition triggered by stress, hormones, foods.
Symptoms: Throbbing headache, nausea, sensitivity to light/sound.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Acute: Paracetamol 500 mg or Sumatriptan 50 mg.
  - Preventive: Propranolol 40 mg daily, Amitriptyline 10 mg at night.
Cure: Manageable but not curable.
Precautions:
  - Avoid triggers (caffeine, irregular sleep, dehydration).
  - Maintain regular sleep, hydration.
  - Use sunglasses/earplugs for sensitivity.

15. Tuberculosis (TB)
Cause: Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily lungs.
Symptoms: Persistent cough, weight loss, night sweats, fever.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Regimen (6 months): Intensive (2 months) Isoniazid + Rifampicin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol; Continuation (4 months) Isoniazid + Rifampicin.
  - Supportive: High-protein diet, monitor liver function.
Cure: Curable with adherence to DOTS program.
Precautions:
  - Follow DOTS protocol.
  - Avoid close contact until non-infectious.
  - Ensure good ventilation.

16. Allergies / Rash / Urticaria
Cause: Allergens (pollen, food), irritants, infections.
Symptoms: Itching, redness, rash, swelling.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antihistamines: Cetirizine 10 mg daily, Levocetirizine 5 mg daily.
  - Topical: Calamine lotion, Hydrocortisone cream (1%).
  - Severe: Prednisolone 20 mg daily (doctor supervision).
Cure: Resolves with treatment; chronic allergies need management.
Precautions:
  - Avoid known allergens.
  - Use hypoallergenic products.
  - Keep skin moisturized.

17. Back Pain / Muscle Pain
Cause: Muscle strain, poor posture, injury.
Symptoms: Localized pain, stiffness, reduced mobility.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Pain relief: Paracetamol 650 mg or Etoricoxib 60 mg daily.
  - Muscle relaxant: Thiocolchicoside 4 mg twice daily.
  - Topical: Diclofenac gel massage.
  - Supportive: Hot fomentation, posture correction, rest.
Cure: Resolves with treatment; chronic cases need management.
Precautions:
  - Maintain proper posture.
  - Avoid heavy lifting.
  - Regular stretching/exercise.

18. Skin Infection / Fungal (e.g., Ringworm, Athlete’s Foot)
Cause: Fungi (dermatophytes).
Symptoms: Itchy, red, scaly patches; nail discoloration.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Topical: Clotrimazole cream twice daily, Terbinafine.
  - Oral (severe): Ketoconazole 200 mg daily for 7 days, Fluconazole, Itraconazole.
Cure: Curable with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Keep skin dry and clean.
  - Avoid sharing towels, shoes.
  - Wear breathable footwear.

19. Sinusitis
Cause: Viral, bacterial, fungal infection; allergies.
Symptoms: Nasal congestion, facial pain, headache, thick discharge.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Viral: Steam inhalation, saline sprays, Oxymetazoline drops (≤5 days).
  - Bacterial: Amoxicillin-Clavulanate 625 mg thrice daily for 7 days.
  - Allergic: Antihistamines, nasal corticosteroids (Fluticasone).
  - Pain relief: Paracetamol.
Cure: Curable (acute); chronic needs management.
Precautions:
  - Avoid allergens/irritants (smoke).
  - Use humidifier.
  - Stay hydrated.

20. Stomach Ulcer / Gastritis
Cause: H. pylori infection, NSAID use, stress.
Symptoms: Burning stomach pain, bloating, nausea, vomiting.
Treatment/Medication:
  - H. pylori: Triple therapy (Amoxicillin, Clarithromycin, Pantoprazole 40 mg).
  - Acid reduction: Pantoprazole 40 mg daily, Sucralfate syrup 10 ml before meals.
Cure: Curable with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Avoid NSAIDs, spicy foods, alcohol.
  - Manage stress.
  - Test for H. pylori if recurrent.

21. Ear Infection (Otitis Media)
Cause: Bacterial or viral infection.
Symptoms: Ear pain, fever, hearing loss, discharge.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antibiotics: Amoxicillin-Clavulanate 625 mg thrice daily for 7 days.
  - Pain relief: Paracetamol.
  - Ear drops: Ciprofloxacin ear drops twice daily.
Cure: Curable with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Avoid water entry in ears.
  - Treat respiratory infections promptly.
  - Seek care for persistent symptoms.

22. Conjunctivitis (Eye Infection)
Cause: Viral, bacterial, or allergic.
Symptoms: Redness, itching, tearing, discharge.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Viral: Artificial tears, cold compress.
  - Bacterial: Moxifloxacin eye drops (1 drop 4 times/day).
  - Allergic: Antihistamine eye drops (Olopatadine).
Cure: Curable (bacterial/allergic); viral resolves in 1–2 weeks.
Precautions:
  - Avoid touching/rubbing eyes.
  - Wash hands frequently.
  - Avoid sharing towels/pillows.

23. Depression / Anxiety (Mild)
Cause: Chemical imbalances, stress, genetics, trauma.
Symptoms: Persistent sadness, loss of interest, excessive worry, restlessness, palpitations.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antidepressants: SSRIs (Sertraline 50 mg, Escitalopram 10 mg daily).
  - Short-term: Benzodiazepines (Clonazepam 0.25 mg as needed).
  - Therapy: Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), counseling.
Cure: Manageable; remission possible with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Seek mental health support.
  - Practice stress management (meditation, yoga).
  - Avoid alcohol, caffeine, drugs.

24. Arthritis / Joint Pain (Osteoarthritis)
Cause: Joint cartilage wear; aging or injury.
Symptoms: Joint pain, stiffness, swelling, reduced mobility.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Pain relief: Aceclofenac 100 mg twice daily, Paracetamol.
  - Supplements: Glucosamine + Chondroitin.
  - Severe: Corticosteroid injections (doctor).
  - Support: Physiotherapy, weight control.
Cure: Manageable but not curable.
Precautions:
  - Maintain healthy weight.
  - Low-impact exercise (swimming).
  - Avoid repetitive joint strain.

25. Epilepsy
Cause: Abnormal brain electrical activity.
Symptoms: Seizures, loss of consciousness, convulsions.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Anticonvulsants: Sodium Valproate 500 mg twice daily, Levetiracetam 500 mg twice daily.
Cure: Manageable with medication.
Precautions:
  - Avoid alcohol, sleep deprivation.
  - Never stop medication suddenly.
  - Regular neurologist follow-up.

26. Hypothyroidism
Cause: Underactive thyroid gland (autoimmune, iodine deficiency).
Symptoms: Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, dry skin, hair loss.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Hormone replacement: Levothyroxine 25–125 mcg daily (empty stomach).
Cure: Manageable with lifelong medication.
Precautions:
  - Monitor TSH every 3 months.
  - Take medication on empty stomach.
  - Avoid coffee/iron supplements with dose.

27. Hyperthyroidism
Cause: Overactive thyroid (Graves’ disease, nodules).
Symptoms: Weight loss, palpitations, sweating, anxiety, heat intolerance.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antithyroid: Carbimazole 5–10 mg thrice daily.
  - Beta-blocker: Propranolol 40 mg for palpitations.
  - Others: Radioactive iodine, surgery.
Cure: Manageable; curable with surgery/radioactive iodine.
Precautions:
  - Monitor thyroid function every 4–6 weeks.
  - Avoid iodine excess.
  - Manage stress.

28. Piles (Hemorrhoids)
Cause: Straining, constipation, prolonged sitting.
Symptoms: Pain, itching, bleeding during bowel movements.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Venotonics: Diosmin + Hesperidin tablets.
  - Stool softener: Lactulose syrup.
  - Topical: Hydrocortisone cream.
  - Supportive: High-fiber diet, sitz baths, hydration.
Cure: Manageable; severe cases may need surgery.
Precautions:
  - Eat high-fiber foods (fruits, vegetables).
  - Avoid straining during bowel movements.
  - Stay hydrated.

29. Obesity
Cause: Excess calorie intake, sedentary lifestyle, genetics.
Symptoms: Excessive body fat, BMI >30.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Lifestyle: Calorie control, regular exercise.
  - Medications: Orlistat 120 mg thrice daily with meals (BMI >30).
  - Severe: Bariatric surgery.
Cure: Manageable with lifestyle changes.
Precautions:
  - Avoid processed, high-calorie foods.
  - Exercise 150 min/week.
  - Monitor weight/BMI.

30. Acne
Cause: Excess oil, clogged pores, bacteria, hormones.
Symptoms: Pimples, blackheads, cysts, redness.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Mild: Benzoyl peroxide gel, Clindamycin gel.
  - Moderate: Doxycycline 100 mg daily (6–8 weeks).
  - Severe: Isotretinoin 20 mg daily (dermatologist).
Cure: Manageable; curable with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Avoid oily cosmetics.
  - Wash face twice daily.
  - Avoid squeezing pimples.

31. Hepatitis (A, B, C)
Cause: Viral infection (HAV, HBV, HCV); A from contaminated food/water, B/C from blood/sexual contact.
Symptoms: Jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, nausea, dark urine.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Hepatitis A: Supportive care (rest, hydration); self-limiting.
  - Hepatitis B: Antivirals (Tenofovir, Entecavir) for chronic cases.
  - Hepatitis C: Direct-acting antivirals (Sofosbuvir, Ledipasvir).
Cure: HAV self-limiting; HBV/HCV curable with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Vaccines for HAV, HBV.
  - Avoid sharing needles, practice safe sex.
  - Ensure safe food/water.

32. Pneumonia
Cause: Bacteria (Streptococcus pneumoniae), viruses, fungi.
Symptoms: Fever, cough, chest pain, shortness of breath, fatigue.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Bacterial: Amoxicillin, Azithromycin, or Levofloxacin.
  - Viral: Supportive care; antivirals for influenza-related cases.
  - Supportive: Oxygen therapy, hydration.
Cure: Curable with treatment; recovery 1–2 weeks.
Precautions:
  - Pneumococcal/influenza vaccines.
  - Avoid smoking, maintain hygiene.
  - Early treatment for respiratory infections.

33. Bronchitis
Cause: Viral (acute) or chronic (smoking, pollution).
Symptoms: Cough, mucus production, wheezing, chest discomfort.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Acute: Symptomatic (rest, hydration, cough syrup).
  - Chronic: Bronchodilators (Salbutamol), inhaled steroids.
Cure: Acute resolves in 1–3 weeks; chronic needs management.
Precautions:
  - Avoid smoking, pollutants.
  - Use humidifier for mucus relief.
  - Vaccination (flu, pneumococcal).

34. Tonsillitis
Cause: Bacterial (Streptococcus) or viral.
Symptoms: Sore throat, difficulty swallowing, fever, swollen tonsils.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Bacterial: Penicillin or Amoxicillin (10 days).
  - Viral: Symptomatic (paracetamol, gargles).
Cure: Curable; bacterial resolves with antibiotics.
Precautions:
  - Avoid sharing utensils.
  - Maintain oral hygiene.
  - Seek care for recurrent tonsillitis.

Symptoms: Itchy, red, dry skin, rash.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Topical: Hydrocortisone (1%), moisturizers.

Cure: Manageable but not curable.

36. Psoriasis

Symptoms: Red, scaly patches, itching, pain.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Topical: Corticosteroids (Betamethasone), vitamin D analogs (Calcipotriene).
  - Systemic: Methotrexate, biologics (Adalimumab).
Cure: Manageable but not curable.
Precautions:
  - Avoid stress, skin injury.
  - Moisturize regularly.
  - Monitor for joint involvement (psoriatic arthritis).

Symptoms: Chronic cough, shortness of breath, wheezing, mucus production.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Bronchodilators: Salbutamol, Tiotropium.

Cure: Manageable but not curable.

38. Kidney Stones
Cause: Crystallization of minerals (calcium, uric acid) in urine.
Symptoms: Severe flank pain, blood in urine, nausea, frequent urination.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Pain relief: Ibuprofen, Tamsulosin (for stone passage).

  - Severe: Surgery (lithotripsy, ureteroscopy).
Cure: Curable; recurrence common.
Precautions:
  - Stay hydrated.
  - Reduce salt, oxalate-rich foods (spinach, nuts).
  - Regular urology follow-up.

Symptoms: Sudden, severe joint pain (big toe), swelling, redness.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Acute: NSAIDs (Indomethacin), Colchicine, corticosteroids.
  - Preventive: Allopurinol, Febuxostat.
Cure: Manageable; acute attacks curable.
Precautions:

  - Limit alcohol (beer, spirits).
  - Stay hydrated.

  - Avoid smoking, excessive alcohol.

1. Shigellosis
Cause: Shigella bacteria via contaminated food/water or fecal-oral route.
Symptoms: Bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, fever, vomiting, tenesmus (urge to pass stool).
Treatment/Medication:
  - Rehydration: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) 1 liter per 3–4 stools.
  - Antibiotics: Azithromycin 500 mg once daily for 3 days or Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 3 days (prescribed).
  - Supportive: Paracetamol for fever, light diet (BRAT: bananas, rice, applesauce, toast).
Cure: Curable with antibiotics and rehydration.
Precautions:
  - Drink boiled or bottled water.
  - Practice good hand hygiene and food safety.
  - Avoid contact with infected individuals.
When to see a doctor: Persistent bloody diarrhea, severe dehydration, or fever >3 days.

2. Amoebiasis (Amoebic Dysentery)
Cause: Entamoeba histolytica parasite via contaminated food/water.
Symptoms: Loose stools with mucus/blood, abdominal pain, vomiting, fatigue, weight loss.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antiparasitic: Metronidazole 500 mg thrice daily for 7–10 days or Tinidazole 2 g daily for 3 days.
  - Luminal agent: Paromomycin 25–35 mg/kg daily for 7 days to clear cysts.
  - Supportive: ORS for hydration, light diet.
Cure: Curable with treatment; relapse possible if untreated.
Precautions:
  - Ensure safe drinking water and food hygiene.
  - Wash hands thoroughly after using the toilet.
  - Avoid raw or undercooked foods in endemic areas.
When to see a doctor: Bloody stools, persistent vomiting, or signs of liver abscess (fever, right upper abdominal pain).

3. Norovirus Infection
Cause: Norovirus via contaminated food, water, or surfaces.
Symptoms: Acute vomiting, watery diarrhea, nausea, abdominal cramps, low-grade fever.
Treatment/Medication:

  - Avoid sex until cleared.

8. Genital Herpes (STI)

Symptoms: Painful blisters, itching, flu-like symptoms.

  - Condoms; avoid contact during outbreaks.
  - Daily suppressive therapy reduces transmission.
  - Disclose status to partners.

1. Jet Lag
Cause: Disruption of circadian rhythm from rapid time zone changes during air travel.
Symptoms: Fatigue, insomnia, irritability, digestive issues, difficulty concentrating.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Symptomatic: Melatonin 0.5–5 mg at bedtime in new time zone.
  - Supportive: Light exposure during day, short naps (<30 min), hydration.
Cure: Self-limiting (resolves in 1–3 days per time zone crossed).
Precautions:
  - Adjust sleep schedule before travel.
  - Avoid alcohol/caffeine near bedtime.
  - Stay active during daylight hours.
When to see a doctor: Persistent symptoms >1 week or severe disruption.

2. Altitude Sickness (Acute Mountain Sickness)
Cause: Low oxygen at high altitudes (>8,000 ft) during mountain travel.
Symptoms: Headache, nausea, dizziness, fatigue, shortness of breath.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Descent: Primary treatment; descend 1,000–2,000 ft if symptoms worsen.
  - Medications: Acetazolamide 125 mg twice daily (prevention), ibuprofen for headache.
  - Supportive: Hydration, rest, oxygen if available.
Cure: Resolves with descent; preventable with acclimatization.
Precautions:
  - Ascend gradually (<1,000 ft/day above 8,000 ft).
  - Avoid alcohol, sedatives; stay hydrated.
  - Pre-travel acetazolamide if high risk.
When to see a doctor: Severe headache, confusion, or worsening symptoms (risk of HAPE/HACE).

3. Heat Exhaustion/Stroke
Cause: Overexposure to high temperatures/humidity during travel in hot climates.
Symptoms: Heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, headache, rapid pulse; stroke includes confusion, seizures.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Cooling: Move to shade, cool cloths, hydration (ORS).
  - Severe: IV fluids, cooling measures in hospital.
Cure: Resolves with prompt cooling; stroke is medical emergency.
Precautions:
  - Wear light clothing, stay hydrated (2–3 liters/day).
  - Avoid midday sun, use sunscreen.
  - Acclimatize gradually.
When to see a doctor: Confusion, vomiting, or temperature >104°F.

4. Sunburn
Cause: UV radiation exposure during outdoor travel without protection.
Symptoms: Red, painful skin, blistering, swelling, fever.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Topical: Aloe vera, hydrocortisone 1% cream.
  - Oral: Ibuprofen 400 mg for pain/inflammation.
  - Supportive: Cool baths, hydration, avoid sun.
Cure: Self-limiting (3–7 days); severe cases may scar.
Precautions:
  - Use SPF 30+ sunscreen, reapply every 2 hours.
  - Wear protective clothing, hat, sunglasses.
  - Avoid peak sun (10 AM–4 PM).
When to see a doctor: Blisters over large area, fever, chills.

5. Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Cause: Blood clots from prolonged immobility during long flights/travel.
Symptoms: Leg swelling, pain, warmth, redness.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Anticoagulants: Enoxaparin (prophylaxis) or rivaroxaban if diagnosed.
  - Supportive: Compression stockings, elevation.
Cure: Treatable; prevents pulmonary embolism.
Precautions:
  - Move every 1–2 hours, calf exercises.
  - Hydrate, avoid alcohol; aspirin if high risk.
  - Compression socks for flights >4 hours.
When to see a doctor: Sudden leg pain/swelling, shortness of breath.

6. Erectile Dysfunction
Cause: Physical (vascular, diabetes, medications), psychological (stress, anxiety), or lifestyle factors.

Treatment/Medication:

Precautions:

When to see a doctor: Persistent issues >3 months or sudden onset.

Cure: Manageable; long-term therapy needed.
Precautions:
  - Avoid accumulating during travel.
  - Set decluttering goals.
  - Seek family support.
When to see a doctor: Clutter impacts safety or relationships.

17. Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)
Cause: Severe childhood trauma, dissociation; stress (e.g., travel) may trigger switches.
Symptoms: Multiple identities, memory gaps, anxiety.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Therapy: Trauma-focused therapy, integration work.
  - Medications: For comorbid anxiety/depression (e.g., SSRIs).
  - Supportive: Grounding techniques, safe spaces.
Cure: Manageable; integration possible for some.
Precautions:
  - Create stable travel routines.
  - Carry grounding objects (e.g., familiar item).
  - Inform travel companions of condition.
When to see a doctor: Frequent dissociation or memory loss.

18. Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)
Cause: Genetics, trauma; relationship or travel stress may worsen.
Symptoms: Emotional instability, impulsivity, fear of abandonment, self-harm.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Therapy: Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), schema therapy.
  - Medications: Mood stabilizers (e.g., lamotrigine 25–200 mg daily).
  - Supportive: Skills training, crisis plans.
Cure: Manageable; significant improvement possible.
Precautions:
  - Develop coping skills for travel stress.
  - Maintain support network.
  - Avoid impulsive decisions.
When to see a doctor: Self-harm or suicidal thoughts.

19. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
Cause: Genetic, neurological; travel disruptions may worsen focus.
Symptoms: Inattention, impulsivity, hyperactivity, disorganization.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Medications: Stimulants (methylphenidate 10–60 mg daily), non-stimulants (atomoxetine 40–80 mg daily).
  - Therapy: Behavioral therapy, organizational skills training.
  - Supportive: Routine, timers for travel tasks.
Cure: Manageable; lifelong condition.
Precautions:
  - Use planners for travel itineraries.
  - Minimize distractions (e.g., noise-canceling headphones).
  - Regular breaks during activities.
When to see a doctor: Difficulty functioning at work or travel.

20. Postpartum Depression
Cause: Hormonal changes, stress post-childbirth; travel or sexual issues may exacerbate.
Symptoms: Severe sadness, fatigue, disinterest in baby, guilt.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Therapy: CBT, interpersonal therapy.
  - Medications: SSRIs (e.g., sertraline 50–100 mg daily, safe for breastfeeding).
  - Supportive: Partner support, rest.
Cure: Manageable; often resolves with treatment.
Precautions:
  - Seek support before travel post-delivery.
  - Prioritize sleep, self-care.
  - Screen for symptoms post-birth.
When to see a doctor: Thoughts of harming self or baby.


1. Neonatal Jaundice (Physiological)
Cause: Immature liver function causing bilirubin buildup; not related to hepatitis.
Symptoms: Yellowing of skin/eyes, lethargy, poor feeding.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Phototherapy: Blue light to break down bilirubin.
  - Supportive: Frequent feeding (breast milk/formula) to promote excretion.
  - Severe: Exchange transfusion if bilirubin >25 mg/dL.
Cure: Self-limiting (resolves in 1–2 weeks); treatable if severe.
Precautions:
  - Monitor skin color in natural light.
  - Ensure 8–12 feeds/day to promote bowel movements.
  - Avoid dehydration in newborns.
When to see a doctor: Jaundice within 24 hours, spreading to arms/legs, or poor feeding.

2. Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
Cause: Surfactant deficiency in preterm infants (<37 weeks).
Symptoms: Rapid breathing, grunting, chest retractions, cyanosis (bluish skin).
Treatment/Medication:
  - Surfactant therapy: Endotracheal surfactant (e.g., poractant alfa).
  - Respiratory support: CPAP or mechanical ventilation.
  - Supportive: Oxygen therapy, IV fluids.
Cure: Treatable; recovery depends on prematurity and treatment.
Precautions:
  - Antenatal steroids (betamethasone) for preterm labor risk.
  - Monitor preterm infants closely post-birth.
  - Ensure NICU access for preterm deliveries.
When to see a doctor: Any breathing difficulty or blue tint in newborn.

3. Neonatal Sepsis
Cause: Bacterial infection (e.g., Group B Streptococcus, E. coli) acquired during birth or postnatally.
Symptoms: Fever, lethargy, poor feeding, respiratory distress, irritability.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Antibiotics: Ampicillin 100 mg/kg IV + gentamicin 4 mg/kg IV for 7–14 days.
  - Supportive: IV fluids, oxygen if needed.
Cure: Curable with early antibiotics; delays risk complications.
Precautions:
  - Maternal GBS screening and intrapartum antibiotics.
  - Maintain sterile delivery environment.
  - Monitor newborn for fever or lethargy.
When to see a doctor: Fever >100.4°F, poor feeding, or lethargy in first month.

4. Congenital Hypothyroidism
Cause: Thyroid gland underdevelopment or maternal iodine deficiency.
Symptoms: Prolonged jaundice, constipation, poor feeding, large tongue, lethargy.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Hormone replacement: Levothyroxine 10–15 mcg/kg/day orally.
  - Supportive: Regular thyroid function monitoring.
Cure: Manageable with lifelong medication; prevents developmental delays.
Precautions:
  - Newborn screening (heel prick test) at 2–5 days.
  - Ensure maternal iodine intake during pregnancy.
  - Administer medication consistently.
When to see a doctor: Missed screening or symptoms like poor growth.

5. Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
Cause: Failure of ductus arteriosus to close after birth, common in preterm infants.
Symptoms: Heart murmur, fast breathing, poor weight gain, feeding difficulties.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Medications: Ibuprofen or indomethacin (IV) to close duct.
  - Surgery: Ligation if medical therapy fails.
  - Supportive: Oxygen, fluid management.
Cure: Curable with treatment; spontaneous closure possible in term infants.
Precautions:
  - Monitor preterm infants for heart issues.
  - Ensure echocardiogram if murmur detected.
  - Support breastfeeding to aid growth.
When to see a doctor: Breathing issues or feeding problems.

6. Necrotizing Enterocolitis (NEC)
Cause: Intestinal inflammation, primarily in preterm infants, linked to feeding or infection.
Symptoms: Bloody stools, abdominal distension, vomiting, lethargy.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Supportive: Stop oral feeds, IV fluids, antibiotics (e.g., vancomycin + piperacillin).
  - Surgery: For intestinal perforation or severe cases.
Cure: Treatable; recovery depends on severity.
Precautions:
  - Gradual introduction of feeds in preterm infants.
  - Breast milk preferred over formula.
  - Monitor for feeding intolerance.
When to see a doctor: Abdominal swelling, bloody stools, or vomiting.

7. Meconium Aspiration Syndrome
Cause: Inhalation of meconium-stained amniotic fluid during birth.
Symptoms: Respiratory distress, cyanosis, barrel-shaped chest.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Respiratory support: Oxygen, CPAP, or ventilation.
  - Antibiotics: If infection suspected (e.g., ampicillin).
  - Supportive: Suctioning at birth, NICU care.
Cure: Treatable; recovery varies with severity.
Precautions:
  - Monitor fetal distress during labor.
  - Avoid aggressive delivery maneuvers.
  - Ensure skilled neonatal team at birth.
When to see a doctor: Breathing difficulty or blue skin post-birth.

8. Hypoglycemia in Newborns
Cause: Low blood sugar from maternal diabetes, prematurity, or delayed feeding.
Symptoms: Jitteriness, lethargy, poor feeding, seizures.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Feeding: Frequent breast milk/formula feeds.
  - IV glucose: Dextrose 10% for severe cases.
  - Supportive: Monitor blood glucose levels.
Cure: Curable with prompt treatment; prevents brain damage.
Precautions:
  - Early and frequent feeding post-birth.
  - Screen infants of diabetic mothers.
  - Monitor preterm or low-birth-weight infants.
When to see a doctor: Seizures, lethargy, or poor feeding.

9. Transient Tachypnea of the Newborn (TTN)
Cause: Delayed lung fluid clearance post-birth, often in C-section or late preterm infants.
Symptoms: Rapid breathing, nasal flaring, mild cyanosis.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Supportive: Oxygen therapy, nasal cannula.
  - Feeding: Continue breast milk/formula as tolerated.
Cure: Self-limiting (resolves in 1–3 days).
Precautions:
  - Monitor respiratory rate post-birth.
  - Encourage vaginal delivery when safe.
  - Ensure warm, humidified environment.
When to see a doctor: Persistent fast breathing >48 hours or worsening.

10. Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)
Cause: Withdrawal from maternal drug use (e.g., opioids) during pregnancy.
Symptoms: Tremors, irritability, poor feeding, seizures, diarrhea.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Medications: Morphine or methadone (tapered doses) for severe withdrawal.
  - Supportive: Swaddling, low-stimulation environment, frequent feeds.
Cure: Treatable; recovery depends on drug exposure.
Precautions:
  - Maternal drug screening during pregnancy.
  - Breastfeeding if mother is stable in treatment.
  - Monitor for withdrawal signs post-birth.
When to see a doctor: Excessive crying, seizures, or poor weight gain.

11. Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)
Cause: Defect in diaphragm allowing abdominal organs into chest, impairing lung development.
Symptoms: Severe respiratory distress, cyanosis, scaphoid abdomen.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Surgery: Repair of diaphragmatic defect post-stabilization.
  - Supportive: Mechanical ventilation, ECMO if severe.
Cure: Treatable with surgery; outcomes vary with lung development.
Precautions:
  - Prenatal ultrasound to detect defect.
  - Deliver at tertiary center with NICU.
  - Avoid early feeding until stabilized.
When to see a doctor: Breathing difficulty at birth or prenatal diagnosis.

12. Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)
Cause: Abnormal retinal blood vessel growth in preterm infants (<32 weeks).
Symptoms: Often asymptomatic; detected via eye exam, may lead to vision loss.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Laser therapy or cryotherapy for severe cases.
  - Anti-VEGF injections (e.g., bevacizumab) in some cases.
  - Supportive: Regular eye exams for preterm infants.
Cure: Treatable; prevents blindness if caught early.
Precautions:
  - Screen preterm infants at 4–6 weeks post-birth.
  - Control oxygen therapy in NICU.
  - Monitor vision development.
When to see a doctor: Missed ROP screening or vision concerns.

13. Intraventricular Hemorrhage (IVH)
Cause: Bleeding in brain ventricles, common in preterm infants (<32 weeks).
Symptoms: Apnea, lethargy, seizures, bulging fontanelle.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Supportive: NICU monitoring, manage intracranial pressure.
  - Medications: Anticonvulsants (e.g., phenobarbital) for seizures.
Cure: Treatable; long-term outcomes vary with severity.
Precautions:
  - Antenatal steroids for preterm labor.
  - Gentle handling of preterm infants.
  - Monitor head circumference.
When to see a doctor: Seizures, lethargy, or abnormal movements.

14. Hyperbilirubinemia (Pathological)
Cause: Excessive bilirubin from hemolysis, infections, or metabolic issues (not physiological jaundice).
Symptoms: Rapid jaundice onset (<24 hours), lethargy, poor feeding.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Phototherapy: Intensive blue light therapy.
  - Exchange transfusion: For severe cases (bilirubin >30 mg/dL).
  - Supportive: Treat underlying cause (e.g., antibiotics for sepsis).
Cure: Treatable; prevents kernicterus if addressed early.
Precautions:
  - Newborn screening for blood type incompatibility.
  - Monitor jaundice progression.
  - Ensure early feeding to reduce bilirubin.
When to see a doctor: Jaundice in first 24 hours or with neurological symptoms.

15. Neonatal Hypocalcemia
Cause: Low calcium levels from prematurity, maternal diabetes, or hypoparathyroidism.
Symptoms: Jitteriness, seizures, irritability, poor feeding.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Calcium supplementation: IV calcium gluconate 10% for acute cases.
  - Supportive: Oral calcium, vitamin D if needed.
Cure: Curable with treatment; addresses underlying cause.
Precautions:
  - Monitor infants of diabetic mothers.
  - Ensure adequate vitamin D in preterm infants.
  - Screen for calcium levels if symptomatic.
When to see a doctor: Seizures or persistent tremors.

16. Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of the Newborn (PPHN)
Cause: Failure of pulmonary vascular relaxation post-birth, often with meconium aspiration.
Symptoms: Severe cyanosis, respiratory distress, low oxygen saturation.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Supportive: Nitric oxide inhalation, ECMO if severe.
  - Medications: Sildenafil for pulmonary vasodilation.
Cure: Treatable; outcomes depend on cause and response.
Precautions:
  - Monitor fetal distress during labor.
  - Ensure advanced NICU care at delivery.
  - Avoid hypoxia during birth.
When to see a doctor: Blue skin or breathing difficulty at birth.

17. Neonatal Polycythemia
Cause: Excess red blood cells, often from placental transfusion or hypoxia.
Symptoms: Ruddy complexion, lethargy, poor feeding, respiratory distress.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Supportive: Partial exchange transfusion for hematocrit >65%.
  - Hydration: IV fluids to reduce viscosity.
Cure: Treatable; resolves with intervention.
Precautions:
  - Monitor infants with delayed cord clamping.
  - Avoid dehydration in newborns.
  - Screen high-risk infants (e.g., small for gestational age).
When to see a doctor: Cyanosis, lethargy, or feeding issues.

18. Apnea of Prematurity
Cause: Immature respiratory control in preterm infants (<34 weeks).
Symptoms: Pauses in breathing >20 seconds, bradycardia, cyanosis.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Medications: Caffeine citrate 20 mg/kg loading dose, then 5–10 mg/kg daily.
  - Supportive: CPAP, nasal oxygen, monitor in NICU.
Cure: Resolves as infant matures (by 36–40 weeks post-conception).
Precautions:
  - Monitor preterm infants for breathing pauses.
  - Maintain warm, stable environment.
  - Ensure NICU care for preterm births.
When to see a doctor: Frequent apneas or cyanosis.

19. Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)
Cause: Genetic enzyme defect (e.g., 21-hydroxylase deficiency) affecting cortisol production.
Symptoms: Ambiguous genitalia (females), vomiting, dehydration, salt-wasting crisis.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Hormones: Hydrocortisone 10–20 mg/m²/day, fludrocortisone for salt loss.
  - Supportive: IV fluids, electrolytes in crises.
Cure: Manageable with lifelong treatment.
Precautions:
  - Newborn screening for CAH.
  - Monitor for adrenal crises during illness.
  - Genetic counseling for parents.
When to see a doctor: Vomiting, dehydration, or genital abnormalities.

20. Gastroschisis
Cause: Congenital abdominal wall defect exposing intestines.
Symptoms: Intestines outside abdomen at birth, poor feeding, infection risk.
Treatment/Medication:
  - Surgery: Primary or staged closure of defect.
  - Supportive: IV nutrition (TPN), antibiotics for infection risk.
Cure: Curable with surgery; long-term bowel issues possible.
Precautions:
  - Prenatal ultrasound for early detection.
  - Deliver at tertiary center with pediatric surgery.
  - Protect exposed intestines at birth (sterile wrap).
When to see a doctor: Prenatal diagnosis or visible defect at birth.


21 Snake Bite
Cause: Bite from venomous snakes (e.g., cobra, viper, krait) or non-venomous snakes.
Symptoms: Pain, swelling, redness at bite site; venomous bites may cause dizziness, nausea, vomiting, difficulty breathing, blurred vision, or paralysis.
First Aid:

Stay calm, keep the bitten person still to slow venom spread.
Lay them down with the bitten area below heart level.
Remove tight clothing, jewelry near bite.
Clean bite with soap and water; do NOT cut or suck wound.
Immobilize bitten limb with a splint or cloth; keep it still.
Do NOT apply ice, tourniquet, or home remedies (e.g., herbs, oils).
Get to a hospital immediately (within 1–2 hours if possible).

Treatment/Medication:

Hospital Care: Anti-snake venom (ASV) given by doctor after identifying snake type (if possible).
Supportive: Pain relief (paracetamol, avoid aspirin/ibuprofen), IV fluids, oxygen if breathing is hard.
Severe Cases: Ventilator for breathing failure, dialysis for kidney damage.

Cure: Curable with timely ASV; delays can cause death or disability.
Precautions:

Wear shoes, long pants in snake-prone areas (fields, forests).
Avoid tall grass, piles of leaves, or dark areas.
Use a stick to check ground before stepping.
Do not handle dead snakes or provoke live ones.

When to See a Doctor: Immediately after bite, even if no symptoms; venom effects may start late.
